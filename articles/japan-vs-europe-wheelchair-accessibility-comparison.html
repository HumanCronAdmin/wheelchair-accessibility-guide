Wheelchair Accessibility in Japan vs. Europe: What Works and What Doesn't

Updated April 14, 2026

Train stations in Tokyo vs. Berlin

First time I rolled off the Narita Express in Tokyo, the elevator situation hit me immediately. Not the lack of elevators—there were plenty. But the elevators were either tucked behind a gate that required assistance to open, or positioned on the opposite side of the platform from where the train doors would open. In Berlin Hauptbahnhof, elevators are right next to the tracks, with tactile markings on the floor leading straight to them. No asking. No waiting. Just go.

The difference isn’t just in design. It’s in the unspoken rules. In Japan, announcements about elevator locations come in Japanese first, then English. In Germany, they switch to English immediately if they sense a foreign accent. One system assumes you’ll adapt. The other adapts to you.

I’ve timed both. In Tokyo’s Shinjuku Station, the elevator to the platform took 4 minutes from the ticket gate. In Berlin’s Hauptbahnhof, it took 90 seconds. Not because the Berlin elevator was faster—because it was placed logically along the most direct route.

Both systems work. One just requires more mental overhead.

Hotel rooms: Ryokan tradition vs. Nordic minimalism

I stayed in a ryokan in Kyoto last year. The tatami room had a traditional low table and futon bedding. The bathroom was down the hall, shared with two other rooms. The wheelchair didn’t fit through the doorway. In Stockholm, the same budget went to a hotel room with a roll-in shower, grab bars, and a door wide enough to clear my chair’s 29-inch frame. The ryokan’s charm didn’t outweigh the daily struggle of negotiating space.

Japanese hotels are improving. The Hyatt Regency Tokyo has accessible rooms with roll-in showers and 36-inch doorways. The Park Hotel Tokyo in Shiodome offers similar specs. But these are exceptions, not the rule. Most mid-range business hotels still treat accessibility as an afterthought. They’ll add a grab bar in the shower, but forget to widen the door.

In Europe, accessibility is baked into the building code from day one. New hotels in Copenhagen or Amsterdam that don’t meet EU standards can’t get a business license. In Japan, the law says public buildings must be accessible, but enforcement is inconsistent. A hotel in Fukuoka might have perfect specs. The one in Sapporo next door might not.

That inconsistency is the real problem. It means every trip requires research, every booking a gamble.

Public toilets: The great equalizer

Both regions get this right. Japan’s accessible toilets are legendary for a reason. They’re spacious, with support bars in multiple positions and sometimes even a bidet function. The one in Tokyo Station’s basement food court has a Western-style toilet, a Japanese-style squat toilet, and a changing table that lowers to wheelchair height. In Amsterdam’s Schiphol Airport, the accessible stall has similar features, plus a fold-down seat for assistance.

But here’s what trips people up. In Japan, the button to open the stall door is often placed too high. If you can’t reach it, you’re stuck. In Europe, the buttons are lower, but the stalls themselves are sometimes too narrow for larger wheelchairs. I’ve had to back out of more than one stall in Berlin because the doorframe wouldn’t clear my chair’s 25-inch width.

The real difference? Maintenance. A Japanese accessible toilet might look pristine when you arrive, but if the flush mechanism is broken, it stays broken for days. In Europe, the same issue gets fixed within hours. It’s not about the initial design. It’s about who’s responsible for keeping it functional.

Taxis and ride-hailing: The gamble of getting from A to B

Japan’s accessible taxis exist, but they’re rare outside major cities. In Tokyo, you can book a Toyota Coaster with a ramp through agencies like Nittan Taxi. The Coaster seats up to 14, with a rear ramp and a wheelchair securement system. The catch? You need to reserve at least 24 hours in advance. And the cost—around ¥15,000 ($100) for a 30-minute ride—isn’t for everyone.

In contrast, Uber in London offers a fleet of wheelchair-accessible vehicles. The Toyota Prius with a rear ramp seats four, and the app shows real-time availability. The price is comparable to a standard UberX. Same in Amsterdam, where wheelchair-accessible options are just another filter in the app.

But here’s the catch I see people miss. In Japan, the accessible taxis are often vans designed for groups, not individuals. If you’re traveling alone, you’re paying for empty seats. In Europe, the vehicles are sized appropriately. One less thing to worry about.

The other mistake? Assuming all ride-hailing apps work the same. In Japan, you can’t just tap “wheelchair accessible” in the app and expect a car to appear. You have to call a specialized agency. In Europe, the filter works. Always check before you book.

Sidewalks and cobblestones: The silent barrier

Kyoto’s historic streets are beautiful. They’re also a nightmare for wheelchair users. The gaps between the stones are uneven. The slopes are steep. The sidewalks—when they exist—end abruptly at intersections with no curb cuts. In contrast, Amsterdam’s sidewalks are flat, wide, and consistently maintained. The cobblestones are set in a grid pattern that’s easier to traverse. Paris is worse than Kyoto in some areas, better in others. It depends on the arrondissement.

The real issue isn’t the material. It’s the expectation. In Europe, accessibility is often seen as a legal requirement. In Japan, it’s treated as a courtesy. That changes how things are built—and who they’re built for.

One pro tip: In Japan, the best sidewalks for wheelchairs are in newer developments like Tokyo’s Toyosu district or Osaka’s Grand Front. The sidewalks are wide, the curbs are flush, and the tactile paving is consistent. If you’re planning a trip, prioritize these areas over older neighborhoods.

Common mistakes people make

First, assuming all accessible features are labeled. In Japan, the international symbol for accessibility is often missing or misused. A toilet might have grab bars but no sign indicating it’s accessible. In Europe, the signs are clearer, but the symbols themselves can be confusing. A blue sign with a white wheelchair doesn’t always mean the entrance is accessible. It might mean there’s an accessible entrance somewhere else.

Second, not checking the fine print on hotel bookings. A room might advertise an “accessible bathroom,” but the doorway could be 28 inches wide. Or the shower might have a 2-inch lip that’s impossible to clear without assistance. Always ask for the exact measurements. And take photos when you arrive. If the room doesn’t match what was promised, you have proof.

What works better depends on the trip

Japan excels in public transportation once you’re on the train. The Shinkansen has accessible cars with priority seating, wide aisles, and accessible restrooms. The stations themselves are another story. Europe is the opposite. Trains can be a nightmare—old stations in Paris, narrow platforms in Rome—but the streets and sidewalks are often better maintained.

If you’re planning a short trip, focus on Europe. The consistency makes it easier to relax. If you’re staying long-term, Japan’s public transport and service culture might outweigh the accessibility inconsistencies. But either way, research is key. And always have a backup plan.

The best places are the ones where accessibility isn’t an afterthought. Where it’s designed into the system from the start. Where the rules are clear, the maintenance is reliable, and the assumptions are made for you—not the other way around.
